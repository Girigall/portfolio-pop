
# SPX Put Credit Spread Strategy, Detailed Summary

## 1. Core Philosophy

This strategy is based on:

- Selling premium on SPX
- Taking advantage of theta decay
- Using high probability setups
- Keeping risk defined
- Trading systematically, not emotionally

The goal is not to hit home runs.  
The goal is to generate consistent income while controlling catastrophic downside.

The edge comes from:

- Probability
- Time decay
- Risk management
- Consistency

Not from prediction.

---

# 2. Instrument

## Underlying

- SPX or SPXW weekly options

Why SPX:

- Extremely liquid
- Tight spreads
- Cash settled
- European style
- No assignment risk before expiration
- Efficient for defined-risk spreads

---

# 3. Strategy Structure

## Standard Structure

Bull Put Credit Spread

You:

- Sell a put
- Buy a lower put

Example:

- Sell 7140 Put
- Buy 7130 Put

You collect a net credit.

---

# 4. Core Parameters

## DTE

- 7 to 14 DTE
- Sweet spot around 10–12 DTE

Reason:

- Fast theta decay
- Good premium
- Enough time to manage

---

## Delta

Short strike:

- 0.10 to 0.15 delta

Typical:

- 0.12 to 0.14

Reason:

- High probability zone
- Good balance between:
    - premium
    - safety
    - decay speed

---

## Spread Width

Preferred:

- 10-point spreads

Occasional:

- 15-point spreads

Avoid:

- Excessively wide spreads unless volatility justifies it

---

## Distance from Price

Usually:

- 4 to 6 percent below SPX

More aggressive:

- 3 to 4 percent

More conservative:

- 6 percent plus

---

## Credit Target

Typical target:

- $0.80 to $1.00 on 10-point spreads

Higher IV environments:

- $1.20 to $1.80 acceptable

---

# 5. Entry Conditions

You prefer entering when:

- Market stabilizes after weakness
- VIX elevated but not panic
- SPX above major support
- No major macro event immediately ahead

You avoid:

- Chasing after large green rallies
- Very low VIX complacency
- Entering during violent breakdowns

## Low VIX

(VIX ~14–16)

- Trade smaller
- Stay further OTM
- More conservative

Reason:

- Premiums compressed
- Risk/reward weaker
- Volatility expansion dangerous

---

## Elevated VIX

(VIX ~20–30)

- Better premium
- Higher edge
- More attractive entries

But:

- Must still respect sizing

---

# 7. Risk Management

This is the most important part of the strategy.

## Profit Taking

Close at:

- 50 to 70 percent of max profit

Typical:

- 65–70 percent

Example:  
Collected:

- $1.00 credit

Close:

- Around $0.30–0.35 debit

Reason:

- Most theta already captured
- Avoid late gamma risk
- Improve consistency

---

## Stop Loss

Hard stop:

- 200 to 250 percent of credit received

Example:  
Collected:

- $1.00

Stop:

- Around $2.00–2.50

This is critical.

The strategy survives because:

- winners are frequent
- losers are controlled

Without stop discipline:

- one trade can erase many wins

---

# 8. Position Sizing

This is currently your weakest area historically.

## Correct sizing

Based on:

- Max spread loss
- Portfolio size
- Total exposure

Example:  
10-point spread:

- max loss ≈ $1,000 per contract

So:

- 5 contracts ≈ $5,000 risk
- 10 contracts ≈ $10,000 risk

The strategy mathematically breaks if sizing becomes too large relative to account size.

---

# 9. Psychological Framework

Main danger:

- Not entries
- Not delta
- Not theta

The real danger:

- Breaking discipline after drawdowns

Typical failure cycle:

1. Winning streak
2. Increase size
3. Ignore stop
4. One large loss
5. Emotional revenge trading

The system only works if:

- rules remain mechanical

# 10. What Makes a Good Trade

A good trade:

- 7–14 DTE
- 0.10–0.15 delta
- 4–6 percent OTM
- Clean liquidity
- Credit around $0.80–1.00+
- Controlled position size
- Defined stop

# 11. What Makes a Bad Trade

Bad trade characteristics:

- Chasing premium
- Too close to spot
- Oversized contracts
- Ignoring stop
- Holding hoping for recovery
- Trading emotionally after loss
- Trading during volatility compression without adjustment
